# AMOS Business And Ecosystem Playbook

Status: working draft

This document is the commercial execution playbook for the AMOS ecosystem. The thesis explains why AMOS should exist. The architecture explains how the system works. The [Ecosystem Flywheel](ecosystem-flywheel.md) explains how commercial activity becomes relay volume and protocol value. This playbook explains how AMOS gets adopted in the real economy.

## Executive Summary

AMOS is live infrastructure for the autonomous economy: a protocol and operating layer where humans and AI agents coordinate work through proof-carrying bounties, Oracle review, reputation, and on-chain settlement.

The business plan is to turn that infrastructure into adoption through two practical ecosystem paths:

1. **Sell and automate**: service providers sell AMOS-powered automation to operating businesses that already have recurring work, administrative drag, and clear ROI pain.
2. **Buy and automate**: acquisition vehicles, operators, or investors acquire, partner with, or co-invest in service-heavy businesses, install AMOS as the operating layer, improve margins and throughput, and either hold for cash flow or exit at a higher multiple.

The near-term wedge is deliberately practical. SMBs, agencies, law firms, construction companies, marketing agencies, and training businesses do not need the full language of bounded autonomous economic organisms. They need a direct promise:

> We automate the work slowing your business down.

Underneath that simple commercial promise, each deployment feeds the same AMOS protocol flywheel:

- Work is mapped into repeatable tasks.
- Tasks become proof-carrying workflows.
- Humans and agents complete work with evidence.
- Oracle and human review improve quality.
- Reusable packages emerge from repeated deployments.
- Verified outcomes become network intelligence.

## Ecosystem Structure

AMOS should present different messages to different markets while preserving one neutral protocol core.

### AMOS Labs

AMOS Labs maintains and advances the neutral core: open-source infrastructure, agent harness, relay, Oracle constitution, token mechanics, on-chain invariants, and long-term autonomous economy thesis.

Primary audience:

- Developers
- Agents
- Protocol contributors
- Strategic investors
- Technical partners
- Long-term ecosystem participants

Message:

> AMOS is open infrastructure for human-aligned autonomous work.

### AMOS Services Co.

AMOS Services Co., if created, is one possible service provider on top of AMOS. It can sell implementation, automation, managed operations, and business transformation services to SMBs and institutional customers, but it should not be treated as the owner of the services layer.

Primary audience:

- SMB owners
- Agencies
- Professional services firms
- Construction and trades companies
- Marketing agencies
- Training businesses
- PE portfolio companies

Message:

> We automate your business without taking judgment out of your hands.

Design principle:

> The services layer is permissionless. Any provider should be able to build, sell, specialize, and compete on top of AMOS.

### Acquisition / Operating Vehicles

Separate acquisition vehicles can buy or partner with operating companies and use AMOS as the transformation layer. These vehicles may be created by AMOS-aligned operators, PE firms, family offices, independent sponsors, or other ecosystem participants.

Primary audience:

- PE firms
- Family offices
- Independent sponsors
- Sellers
- Lenders
- Co-investors

Message:

> We use AMOS to turn under-systematized operating businesses into more efficient, measurable, and scalable companies.

## Path 1: Sell And Automate

### Thesis

Most SMBs are not limited by ideas. They are limited by coordination cost, owner dependency, messy systems, repetitive administrative work, poor reporting, and slow follow-through.

AMOS can be sold as a practical automation partner before the customer needs to understand the full protocol.

### Ideal Customer Profile

Strong first-fit customers have:

- $1M-$25M revenue
- Repetitive workflows
- Administrative bottlenecks
- Existing staff who understand the business
- Weak or fragmented software systems
- Owner or manager time trapped in coordination
- Clear value from faster quoting, follow-up, reporting, onboarding, training, or compliance

Current promising segments:

- Continuing education and training companies
- Construction companies
- Specialty trades
- Law firms and legal support businesses
- Marketing agencies
- Field services
- Compliance-heavy local services

### Offer Ladder

#### 1. Automation Audit

Purpose: map the business and identify high-ROI workflows.

Deliverables:

- Workflow inventory
- Systems map
- Automation opportunity scorecard
- Risk and approval map
- 30/60/90-day deployment plan

Estimated pricing:

- Small business: $2.5K-$7.5K
- Mid-market SMB: $7.5K-$25K
- PE portfolio or multi-location operator: $25K-$75K

#### 2. AMOS Implementation

Purpose: install AMOS around the highest-value workflows.

Deliverables:

- Harness setup
- Data and tool connections
- Workflow automation
- Human approval paths
- Reporting dashboards
- SOP and knowledge-base ingestion
- Proof-carrying task loops for critical workflows

Estimated pricing:

- Focused implementation: $15K-$50K
- Department implementation: $50K-$150K
- Full operating layer: $150K-$500K+

#### 3. Managed Automation

Purpose: keep the system improving after launch.

Deliverables:

- Ongoing workflow monitoring
- Agent task management
- Reporting
- QA and review
- New automation opportunities
- Staff enablement
- Package upgrades

Estimated pricing:

- Small business: $2K-$7.5K/month
- Growth SMB: $7.5K-$25K/month
- Portfolio or institutional customer: $25K-$100K/month

### Sales Motion

The services company should sell simple outcomes:

- Reduce admin time
- Improve response speed
- Increase quote throughput
- Improve compliance readiness
- Make reporting real-time
- Reduce owner dependency
- Capture institutional knowledge
- Let staff do higher-value work

Avoid leading with:

- RSI
- Autonomous organism
- Token economy
- Blockchain
- Agent protocol internals

Those are real, but they belong underneath the customer-facing product.

### Success Metrics

Each customer deployment should measure:

- Admin hours removed
- Revenue per employee
- Quote-to-close time
- Customer response time
- Reporting cycle time
- Completion rate for recurring tasks
- Gross margin improvement
- Owner hours removed from daily operations
- Number of workflows converted into reusable packages

## Path 2: Buy And Automate

### Thesis

Many distressed or under-optimized companies are not fundamentally bad businesses. They are under-systematized businesses with weak process, poor reporting, founder dependency, slow follow-up, and high coordination cost.

AMOS can become the operating layer that makes these companies healthier.

The acquisition thesis:

> Buy or partner into service-heavy businesses at modest valuations, install AMOS, improve operating performance, and either hold the resulting cash flow or sell the improved business at a higher quality multiple.

### Target Profile

Ideal first acquisition or co-investment target:

- Revenue: $1M-$5M
- EBITDA/SDE: $150K-$750K
- Purchase price: roughly 2.5x-5x SDE/EBITDA depending on quality
- Owner wants exit, partial exit, or operational relief
- Demand exists but operations are messy
- Workflows are repetitive
- Staff or contractors preserve domain knowledge
- Business is not dependent on one irreplaceable relationship
- There is a clear 100-day automation plan

Larger platform target:

- Revenue: $5M-$25M
- EBITDA: $750K-$3M
- Purchase price: roughly 4x-7x EBITDA for many service businesses, with higher multiples for recurring revenue, low owner dependency, and strong growth

### Best Early Verticals

Good early targets:

- Training and compliance businesses
- Construction services
- Specialty trades
- Marketing agencies
- Legal support services
- Back-office-heavy professional services
- Local services with repeatable workflows
- Fragmented businesses with real demand and weak systems

Avoid early:

- Businesses with broken demand
- Businesses with unclear books
- Heavy regulatory risk without a review playbook
- Businesses dependent on one founder relationship
- Businesses needing large capital expenditure before automation can matter
- Turnarounds where the core product is not wanted by the market

### Deal Sourcing Plan

Channels:

- PE firm relationships
- Independent sponsor networks
- Business brokers
- SBA lenders
- CPA and attorney referral networks
- Founder outreach
- Industry associations
- Existing AMOS service customers
- Operators who want partial liquidity and operational help

Estimated annual sourcing costs:

- Broker/listing access and databases: $5K-$25K
- Travel and seller meetings: $10K-$50K
- Legal templates and entity setup: $5K-$25K
- Deal screening support: $10K-$75K
- Advisor/referral fees: success-based or 1%-5% depending on deal type

The early goal is not volume. The goal is a narrow pipeline of companies where AMOS has obvious operational leverage.

### Diligence Scorecard

Every candidate should be scored on:

- Revenue durability
- Gross margin
- Customer concentration
- Owner dependency
- Staff quality
- Workflow repeatability
- Data availability
- Systems fragmentation
- Regulatory risk
- Litigation or liability risk
- Working capital needs
- Automation upside
- 100-day impact potential
- Exit optionality

Red flags:

- No reliable financials
- Unclear tax or payroll exposure
- Customer concentration above acceptable limits
- Key employees likely to leave
- No repeatable sales motion
- Major compliance exposure
- Seller unwilling to support transition

### Acquisition Cost Model

Small first deal example:

| Item | Estimate |
| --- | ---: |
| Purchase price | $500K-$2.5M |
| Equity/down payment | 10%-30% |
| Legal | $15K-$75K |
| Accounting / QoE / diligence | $10K-$75K |
| AMOS deployment | $25K-$150K |
| Working capital cushion | $50K-$300K |
| First 90-day operating support | $50K-$250K |

Practical cash required:

- Lean small deal: $150K-$350K
- More serious first deal: $500K-$1.5M
- PE-backed platform deal: $2M+ equity and reserve capacity

These are planning ranges, not financing advice. Actual costs depend on deal size, leverage, lender requirements, diligence depth, working capital needs, and regulatory exposure.

### First 100-Day Turnaround Playbook

#### Days 0-15: Stabilize And Map

- Secure books, systems, credentials, and contracts
- Interview owner, staff, customers, and vendors
- Map revenue lines and operational bottlenecks
- Identify urgent cash, collections, and service issues
- Build AMOS workflow inventory
- Freeze risky process changes until the business is understood

#### Days 16-45: Install Operating Visibility

- Deploy AMOS harness
- Connect email, documents, CRM, finance, scheduling, and task systems where appropriate
- Build reporting dashboards
- Create SOP and knowledge-base layer
- Establish human approval paths
- Convert the first 5-10 workflows into AMOS-managed loops

#### Days 46-75: Automate High-ROI Work

- Quote/proposal generation
- Follow-up sequences
- Customer intake
- Scheduling support
- Invoice and collections support
- Training/onboarding workflows
- Compliance documentation
- Internal reporting

#### Days 76-100: Scale And Package

- Measure operational improvements
- Remove duplicated software or manual steps
- Train staff into new workflows
- Create reusable package components
- Identify next automation wave
- Decide hold, grow, roll-up, or prepare for exit

### Value Creation Levers

AMOS can create value by improving:

- EBITDA margin
- Revenue per employee
- Sales throughput
- Customer response speed
- Collections cycle
- Owner independence
- Reporting quality
- Staff onboarding
- Compliance readiness
- Repeatability of service delivery

The most important multiple-expansion lever is reducing owner dependency and proving the company can operate through systems.

## Public Safety And eLearning Vertical

### Thesis

The continuing education company is the first trusted institutional vertical for AMOS.

Start with the wedge agencies already understand:

> Continuing education, certification tracking, and audit-ready training records for public safety agencies.

Then expand into the broader operating layer:

> AMOS helps agencies reduce administrative burden, preserve institutional knowledge, and coordinate verified work with human oversight.

### Phase 1: LMS Trust Beachhead

Core features:

- Course delivery
- Officer accounts
- Agency dashboards
- Certification tracking
- Mandated continuing education workflows
- Completion evidence
- Audit exports
- Role-based reporting
- Policy acknowledgment
- Scenario-based training modules

Primary buyers:

- Police departments
- Sheriff's offices
- Municipal agencies
- Training coordinators
- Chiefs and command staff
- State or regional training bodies

### Phase 2: Agency Operations Layer

Expansion workflows:

- Policy updates and comprehension checks
- SOP management
- Internal knowledge base
- Grant writing support
- Training needs analysis
- Onboarding and field training workflows
- Equipment and procurement documentation
- Compliance calendar
- Accreditation support
- Annual training plans

Positioning:

> The LMS becomes the system of record for training readiness. AMOS extends that into operational readiness.

### Phase 3: AI-Augmented Officer Support

Appropriate early uses:

- Training simulations
- Report writing coaching
- Policy lookup
- Legal update summaries
- Scenario debriefs
- Supervisor review assistance
- Officer wellness resource navigation
- Role-specific learning paths

Hard boundary:

> AMOS supports training, documentation, readiness, and review. It does not automate coercive judgment.

Avoid early:

- Real-time enforcement recommendations
- Predictive policing
- Use-of-force decision automation
- Investigative decisions without strict policy and legal review
- Any workflow where AI appears to replace officer judgment

### Phase 4: Public Safety Work Marketplace

Once agencies are active, AMOS can coordinate shared work:

- Create a training module
- Review a policy update
- Summarize a legal change
- Build a scenario
- Translate training content
- Convert documents into courses
- Create grant templates
- Build accreditation checklists
- Analyze training gaps
- Draft SOP comparison summaries

Participants:

- Officers
- Retired officers
- Trainers
- Attorneys
- Policy experts
- AI agents
- AMOS reviewers
- Agencies

This becomes a specialized public safety knowledge and work network.

## Product Flywheel

The key strategic advantage is that services revenue, acquisitions, and protocol development all reinforce each other.

```text
Customer deployments
  -> workflow maps
  -> proof-carrying task loops
  -> reusable packages
  -> better agent performance
  -> stronger Oracle review
  -> more measurable outcomes
  -> easier sales and acquisitions
  -> more deployments
```

Every business AMOS automates should produce:

- New workflow templates
- New package capabilities
- New proof receipt patterns
- New validation plans
- New failure capsules
- New training data for review quality
- New ROI evidence

## Risk Register

### Message Confusion

Risk: SMBs do not care about the grand protocol thesis.

Control: separate AMOS Labs protocol messaging from AMOS Services Co. customer messaging.

### Over-Automation In Sensitive Domains

Risk: public safety customers reject AI if it appears to automate officer judgment.

Control: focus on training, compliance, documentation, readiness, and review. Keep coercive judgment out of scope.

### Services Drag

Risk: services revenue becomes custom consulting that does not improve the product.

Control: every services project must produce reusable workflows, package capabilities, proof receipt patterns, or case-study evidence.

### Bad Acquisition

Risk: AMOS buys a business with broken demand, hidden liabilities, or unrecoverable culture issues.

Control: strict diligence scorecard, small first deal, transition support, and no acquisition where automation is the only thing that can save the business.

### Protocol Distraction

Risk: customer-facing demands pull the team away from the core autonomous loop.

Control: prioritize deployments that exercise the same harness, relay, proof receipt, Oracle, and package layers.
